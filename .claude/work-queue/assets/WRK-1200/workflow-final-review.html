A strategic prioritization skill that replaces ad-hoc "what's next?" decisions with a structured framework. It should:

  1. Classify work into tracks:
  2. Harness — work-queue, skills, CI/CD, agent tooling, ecosystem plumbing
  3. Engineering — digitalmodel, assetutilities, worldenergydata, assethold, OGManufacturing calculations/features

  4. Market readiness — demos, documentation, aceengineer-website, presentation artifacts, industry-facing outputs

  5. Score and rank pending WRK items against:

  6. Market impact (does this move us closer to presenting to O&G industry?)
  7. Engineering value (does this build reusable calculation capability?)
  8. Harness necessity (is this blocking engineering or market work?)
  9. Dependency chain (what does this unblock?)

  10. Effort vs payoff ratio

  11. Enforce a healthy balance — harness work is necessary but should not dominate; the skill should flag when harness WRKs exceed a configurable threshold (e.g. >40% of active work) and redirect toward engineering/market items

  12. Race-to-market lens — every recommendation should answer: "How does this help us present our engineering tools to the oil and gas industry sooner?"

  13. Output a prioritized shortlist — top 3-5 items across all tracks with rationale, not just a sorted list

  14. Visionary filter — apply product visionary principles to every recommendation:

  15. Jobs: "Focus is about saying no" — actively recommend WRK items to defer, descope, or kill. A 450-item queue is a symptom of saying yes to everything. The skill should trim ruthlessly.
  16. Jobs: "Start with the customer experience and work backwards" — an O&G engineer using our tools should feel delight, not see our internal plumbing. Prioritize what the user touches.
  17. Musk: "First principles thinking" — when scoring, ask "does this need to exist at all?" before asking "how important is it?" Eliminate before prioritizing.
  18. Ive: "Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication" — prefer one polished, demo-ready module over five half-finished ones. Depth over breadth.
  19. Grove: "Only the paranoid survive" — identify the strategic inflection point: what is the one capability that, if we ship it now, changes how the industry sees us?

  20. Bezos: "Work backwards from the press release" — for market-track items, frame the recommendation as "what would the announcement say?" If there's no compelling announcement, it's not market-ready work.

  21. The "demo in 30 seconds" test — every top recommendation must pass: "Can an O&G engineer see the value of this in a 30-second demo?" If not, it's infrastructure, not product.

Why — - The workspace has ~450 WRK items; without strategic filtering, harness/tooling work absorbs all capacity - The actual value is in engineer...
  • The workspace has ~450 WRK items; without strategic filtering, harness/tooling work absorbs all capacity
  • The actual value is in engineering modules (CP, fatigue, hydrodynamics, energy data, asset analysis) — these are what the O&G industry cares about
  • A marketing milestone (conference, demo, website launch) creates a forcing function that aligns all work streams
  • The skill encodes product management discipline so every session starts with "what moves the needle most?" rather than "what's next in the queue?"
  • Great products are built by people who say no more than they say yes — this skill is the mechanism for saying no to harness work that doesn't serve the end user
  • The O&G industry judges tools by what they do, not how they're built — our internal sophistication means nothing if the demo isn't compelling
